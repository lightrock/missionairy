Proposed Title:
MissionAiry: A Finite Mission-Closure Adaptor for Controlled Emergence in Heterogeneous AI Collectives

Abstract:
MediaNow, Inc. proposes MissionAiry, a TA1/TA2 DICE adaptor architecture for decentralized coordination and local inference control across heterogeneous AI agents operating under contested, degraded, and adversarial mission conditions. The core technical claim is that controlled emergence requires each agent to expose not only capability and state, but also finite, inspectable commitments: what the agent is authorized to do, what evidence it is relying on, what command scope it is operating under, what quality of service it can sustain, and when its local inference context must expire, degrade, or be pruned.

MissionAiry develops this as a finite mission-closure layer built from two complementary overlays. PFEM, the Polycentric Federated Evidence Mesh, preserves evidence provenance, confidence, contradiction state, freshness, and audit receipts across local agents and rollup views. PFCOM, the Polycentric Federated Command Mesh, preserves tasking, authority, role commitments, execution gates, fallback conditions, and accountability. Together, these overlays give heterogeneous agents a common mission grammar without requiring a central orchestrator or identical internal models.

The proposed TA1 contribution is a peer-to-peer coordination method in which agents form local mission teams through bounded role offers, capability commitments, evidence receipts, and quality-of-service declarations. Agents may bid for roles, reject roles, delegate subtasks, or trigger replanning only when local closure conditions are satisfied. This supports distributed mission decomposition, resilient task reassignment after agent loss, and containment of compromised or stale peers without full global replanning.

The proposed TA2 contribution is a local inference-control method based on QLF/ZFA-style finite closure. QLF supplies a formal language for admissible mission states, while ZFA, Zero Free Action, enforces that no agent action is treated as free: every proposed action must close against capability, authority, evidence, energy, communication state, time, consequence, and command scope. This gives TA1 an actionable invariant: an agent’s local reasoning may remain cognitively agile, but mission-relevant outputs must carry closure receipts and explicit expiration/degrade conditions.

MissionAiry will evaluate the adaptor in simulation using heterogeneous mission agents representing mobile platforms, sensors, communications relays, logistics resources, human authority nodes, local policy agents, evidence agents, and adversary/failure agents. Initial scenarios will emphasize critical-infrastructure airspace status and contested mission support because these domains naturally require decentralized evidence fusion, local policy, intermittent communications, and independent rollup reasoning. Evaluation will compare the MissionAiry adaptor against centralized orchestration baselines across scalability, time-to-recovery, mission alignment, role coherence, adversarial robustness, and useful cognitive diversity.
