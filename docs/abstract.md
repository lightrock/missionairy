Proposed Title:
MissionAiry: An AI Mind-Agnostic Mission Compiler for Controlled Emergence in Heterogeneous AI Collectives

Abstract:
MediaNow, Inc. proposes MissionAiry, an AI mind-agnostic mission compiler and local adaptor architecture for TA1/TA2 DICE decentralized coordination and local inference control across heterogeneous AI agents operating under contested, degraded, and adversarial mission conditions. The core innovation is a finite mission-closure boundary that compiles commander intent, local policy, evidence requirements, capability declarations, and quality-of-service constraints into inspectable mission packets. Those packets instantiate room-scoped mission cells: bounded peer process spaces in which agent identities, role offers, claims, handoffs, and rollup summaries are treated as capability-bearing mission events rather than ordinary messages. Before an agent role, peer message, plan update, or inference output can affect collective behavior, the adaptor requires a closure receipt against authority, evidence, capability, energy, communications, time, consequence, role, and quality of service. This gives TA2 a measurable local-control object and gives TA1 an actionable invariant: agent reasoning may remain diverse and cognitively agile, but mission-relevant commitments are bounded, expiring, auditable, and revocable.

Within each room-scoped mission cell, MissionAiry implements this boundary through two complementary state layers. PFEM, the Polycentric Federated Evidence Mesh, is the evidence and claim layer: it preserves provenance, confidence, contradiction state, freshness, and audit receipts so the cell can determine what it is allowed to believe and what may be safely summarized upward. PFCOM, the Polycentric Federated Command Mesh, is the authority and action layer: it preserves tasking, authority, role commitments, execution gates, fallback conditions, and accountability so the cell can determine what it is allowed to do. Together, PFEM and PFCOM provide a common mission grammar while allowing different reasoning engines, autonomy stacks, sensors, platforms, logistics resources, and human authority nodes to participate through the same adaptor interface. Quantum-OS-style peer rooms provide the coordination pattern: each local team is a bounded shared process, each role or resource is a transferable capability, and cross-cell rollup occurs only through explicit bridge peers carrying closure receipts.

The proposed TA1 contribution is a peer-to-peer coordination method in which agents form local mission teams through bounded role offers, capability commitments, evidence receipts, and quality-of-service declarations. Agents may bid for roles, reject roles, delegate subtasks, or trigger replanning only when local closure conditions are satisfied. Multi-agent team formation is modeled as a rendezvous-style composite commitment: sensing, relay, compute, logistics, authority, and policy contributions must close together, or the proposed team does not become operational. The TA1 planner consumes TA2-exposed validity intervals, role-coherence bounds, and expiration/degrade conditions to set planning horizons and replanning schedules. This supports distributed mission decomposition, resilient task reassignment after agent loss, and containment of compromised, stale, or contradictory peers without full global replanning.

The proposed TA2 contribution is a local inference-control method based on QLF/ZFA-style finite closure. QLF supplies the admissible-state grammar: mission reality is represented as finite, inspectable distinctions among evidence, authority, role, capability, time, energy, communications, consequence, and command scope. ZFA, Zero Free Action, supplies the gate: no agent action is treated as free, and no mission-state transition is accepted unless the claimed change closes against the required mission accounts. The formal object is a mission commitment record that binds a proposed output to its evidence, authority, role, validity interval, degrade rule, and revocation condition. This allows local inference control to operate across large models, small models, rule systems, planners, hybrid autonomy stacks, and degraded controllers without requiring access to identical internal representations.

MissionAiry will evaluate the adaptor using an internal evaluation harness designed for compatibility with DICE TA3 integration. The harness separates degradation of the AI reasoning layer from degradation of the hardware/platform layer, enabling controlled tests of role drift, hallucinated or unsupported claims, compromised agents, stale context, degraded sensors, intermittent communications, failing platforms, and partial command updates. The simulation will include heterogeneous mission agents representing mobile platforms, sensors, communications relays, logistics resources, human authority nodes, local policy agents, evidence agents, and adversary/failure agents. Initial scenarios will emphasize sustained contested mission support: distributed sensing, communications continuity, logistics status, local authority constraints, mission updates, and independent rollup reasoning under degraded trust and intermittent connectivity. This framing keeps the evaluation generalizable across defense-relevant mission settings rather than tying the adaptor to a single domain or platform class. Evaluation will compare MissionAiry against centralized orchestration baselines across scalability, time-to-recovery, mission alignment, role coherence, adversarial robustness, and useful cognitive diversity.
