Proposed Title:
MissionAiry: An AI Mind-Agnostic Mission-Closure Adaptor for Controlled Emergence in Heterogeneous AI Collectives

Abstract:
MediaNow, Inc. proposes MissionAiry, an AI mind-agnostic adaptor architecture for TA1/TA2 DICE decentralized coordination and local inference control across heterogeneous AI agents operating under contested, degraded, and adversarial mission conditions. The core technical claim is that controlled emergence requires each agent-facing reasoning layer and platform layer to expose not only capability and state, but also finite, inspectable commitments: what the agent is authorized to do, what evidence it is relying on, what command scope it is operating under, what quality of service it can sustain, and when its local inference context must expire, degrade, or be pruned. By remaining AI mind-agnostic, MissionAiry separates mission closure from agent implementation: the adaptor governs evidence, authority, quality of service, role commitments, and expiration/degrade conditions without assuming identical internal models, identical hardware, or a single central orchestrator.

MissionAiry develops this approach as a finite mission-closure logic layer built from two complementary overlays. PFEM, the Polycentric Federated Evidence Mesh, preserves evidence provenance, confidence, contradiction state, freshness, and audit receipts across local agents and rollup views. PFCOM, the Polycentric Federated Command Mesh, is the command-side complement to PFEM, preserving tasking, authority, role commitments, execution gates, fallback conditions, and accountability. Together, these overlays give heterogeneous agents a common mission grammar while allowing different reasoning engines, autonomy stacks, sensors, platforms, and human authority nodes to participate through the same adaptor boundary.

The proposed TA1 contribution is a peer-to-peer coordination method in which agents form local mission teams through bounded role offers, capability commitments, evidence receipts, and quality-of-service declarations. Agents may bid for roles, reject roles, delegate subtasks, or trigger replanning only when local closure conditions are satisfied. This supports distributed mission decomposition, resilient task reassignment after agent loss, and containment of compromised, stale, or contradictory peers without full global replanning.

The proposed TA2 contribution is a local inference-control method based on QLF/ZFA-style finite closure. QLF supplies a formal language for admissible mission states, while ZFA, Zero Free Action, enforces that no agent action is treated as free: every proposed action must close against capability, authority, evidence, energy, communication state, time, consequence, and command scope. This gives TA1 an actionable invariant: an agent's local reasoning may remain cognitively agile, but mission-relevant outputs must carry closure receipts and explicit expiration/degrade conditions.

MissionAiry will evaluate the adaptor in a simulation environment that separates degradation of the AI reasoning layer from degradation of the hardware/platform layer. This enables controlled tests of large models, small models, rule systems, planners, hallucinating agents, compromised agents, degraded sensors, intermittent communications, failing platforms, and partial command updates. The simulation will include heterogeneous mission agents representing mobile platforms, sensors, communications relays, logistics resources, human authority nodes, local policy agents, evidence agents, and adversary/failure agents. A mission compiler will close proposed missions and mission updates under finite logic before deployment, including cases where long time horizons, degraded communications, and distributed command authority require partial or asynchronous updates from more than one commander or location. Initial scenarios may emphasize forward critical-infrastructure status and contested mission support because these defensive, long-horizon domains require decentralized evidence fusion, local policy, intermittent communications, and independent rollup reasoning. Evaluation will compare the MissionAiry adaptor against centralized orchestration baselines across scalability, time-to-recovery, mission alignment, role coherence, adversarial robustness, and useful cognitive diversity.

The result is a concrete DICE research path: a local adaptor that lets simulated heterogeneous AI agents coordinate, adapt, and recover through controlled emergence while preserving mission authority, evidence traceability, and human-command intent.
